KPIs & big numbers

Sometimes the most effective chart is barely a chart at all — a single number, set large, that answers the one question a reader came with. On a dashboard it is the anchor: the figure the eye lands on first, before it works through the supporting detail. The job of this family is headline, not exploration.

The big number (BAN)

A BAN — Big Aggregate Number (or, less politely, Big A** Number) — is a headline metric shown large, with just enough context to be read at a glance: a comparison and a small trend. It is the dashboard equivalent of a topline sentence.

The number does the work; everything else is there to keep it honest. A bare “55%” tells you nothing about whether that is good, improving, or alarming — so the tile adds a label (what is being measured, and when), a change cue (▲ 0.8 pts vs 2024), and a sparkline (the path that got us here). That is the whole idea: context is what turns a number into a decision.

Anatomy of a big number

A well-built BAN has five parts, and no more:

  • The label — what this measures and over what period. Small, grey, above the number. (“Grade-level proficiency · 2025.”)
  • The number — the indicator itself, by far the largest element. This is the only thing a reader is guaranteed to take away.
  • The comparison — the figure on its own is just a value; pair it with a previous period, a target, or a goal so the reader knows whether to be pleased.
  • The change cue — the one colored element. Teal for a rise, rust for a fall. Color earns its place here because direction is the judgment.
  • The supporting micro-chart — a sparkline or tiny bar that shows the trend without axes or labels. It answers “is this a blip or a pattern?”

Color only the change cue. In the example above, the label is grey, the number is near-black, and only the ▲ change is colored. One accent keeps the eye on the single thing that carries a verdict. A BAN wearing four colors is a BAN with no focus.

Ten principles for a big number that works

NCDPI’s BAN guidance adapts Aurélien Vautier’s “Big Numbers” framework — the single best treatment of this pattern we’ve found. The four words he opens with are the test every tile should pass: a big number should be actionable, accessible, attainable, and accurate. His ten principles, in our words:

  1. Positioning — place big numbers prominently: the top, or the left, where the reading eye starts (see our dashboard layout guidance on the Z-pattern).
  2. Key insight — a BAN should carry a number worth deciding on, not just a number that was easy to compute. If no one acts on it, it doesn’t earn the size.
  3. Minimalist — clean, simple, free of clutter. The number is the design.
  4. Supporting visuals — pair the number with a small chart (a sparkline, a bar) to show trend or change over time.
  5. Perspective — give the number a frame of reference: a prior period, a target, a goal. This is the difference between “55%” and “55%, up from 45% in 2021.”
  6. Exploration — on an interactive dashboard, a BAN can be an entry point — click it to drill into the detail behind it, or let it reflect the current filter.
  7. Color with care — use color sparingly, to flag a change or an alert, never as decoration (NCDPI: this is the same rule as our color conventions — color means something).
  8. Limit the quantity — a wall of big numbers is no big number at all. Keep a dashboard to a few, so each can still command attention.
  9. Formatting — standardize fonts, decimals, and units across every tile so the set reads as one system. (Round to whole points unless a fraction is the message.)
  10. Actionable — let the number lead somewhere: a drill-down, a linked view, the data behind it. The big number is the start of the question, not the end.

Four ways to add context

The same metric can be dressed for different jobs. Vautier names four common variations — each adds a different kind of context to the headline figure:

  • The Versus — two numbers side by side (this year vs. last), so the reader makes the comparison directly.
  • The Storyteller — the number plus a short sentence of narrative (“up 0.8 points; on pace to clear 56% next year”). Best when the so-what needs spelling out.
  • The Textual Versus — the comparison folded into a compact line of text rather than two large figures. Quietest of the four; good when space is tight.
  • The Bullet graph — the number measured against a target in a small bar. When the question is “are we hitting the goal?”, this is the strongest of the four — and it’s a chart type in its own right (below).

The bullet chart

A bullet chart is the natural next step from a BAN: it keeps the compact, glance-able footprint but adds an explicit target. The bar is the actual value; the tick is the goal; the faint background band gives a qualitative range. It answers “did we hit it?” for a whole list of categories at once.

The bullet chart also appears under difference from a baseline — actual-vs-target is a deviation story as much as a KPI one. Same chart, two jobs.

NCDPI specifics

  • Label school years by the spring. Write 2025, not “2024–25” — the BAN has no room to explain a hyphenated range, and the spring year is our house convention.
  • Valence lives on the change cue, not the number. The proficiency figure itself is near-black; teal/rust is reserved for the direction of change, where a judgment is actually being made.

The ten principles and the four variations on this page adapt Aurélien Vautier’s “10 Commandments of Big Numbers” framework — see his work at datavizclarity.com. To see the four variations live and interactive, Felipe Sebben rebuilt them faithfully in Tableau: The 10 Commandments of Big Numbers in Practice. The NCDPI examples above use real, public accountability data and the NCDPI brand palette.
