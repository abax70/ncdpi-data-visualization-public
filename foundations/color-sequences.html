Color — sequences

Use a sequential palette when the data has a natural order from low to high. NCDPI provides three, because in education “high” isn’t always “good”:

  • Sequence to Good (teal) — higher = better (proficiency, growth met).
  • Sequence to Bad (rust) — higher = worse (chronic absenteeism, suspensions).
  • Sequence to Neutral (blue) — ordered, but with no good/bad meaning. Runs light grey → Category-1 blue #5085BC. Use it for a ranked or binned measure that shouldn’t read as better or worse — a count of schools by district, enrollment bands, years of experience.

Darker always means more — the dark-is-more convention people read intuitively.

Explore the palettes

Pick a direction and the number of steps:

The steps are equally spaced in color space. Each step is the same perceptual distance from the next, from the neutral end to the full color. So the values they stand for should be equally spaced too — even color steps imply even bins (0–20, 20–40, …), not arbitrary cutoffs.

Don’t let color do all the work. Adjacent steps are close in lightness and won’t all meet WCAG contrast against each other. Keep a labeled legend or value labels, and add a thin white border where segments touch (pie, treemap, stacked, map).

Continuous variants. Each sequence also has a smooth, stepless continuous ramp. Reach for it only where the data is truly continuous and position can’t carry the magnitude — almost always a choropleth map or a heatmap. For anything with countable categories, use the stepped palettes above.

Example

A sequential palette earns its keep where position can’t show magnitude — here a heatmap, where color carries the value. Match the palette to the meaning: a proficiency view uses Sequence to Good; shade a chronic-absenteeism view with Sequence to Bad.
